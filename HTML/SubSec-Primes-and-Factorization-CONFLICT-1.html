Section 1.3 Primes and Factorization

¶

As described in the Introduction, our main goal is to build a deep structural understanding of the notion of factorization. That is, splitting objects (e.g., numbers, polynomials, matrices) into products of other numbers. One of the most familiar examples of this process involves factoring integers into products of primes.

Definition 1.3.1.

Let \(p > 1\) be a natural number. We say \(p\) is prime if whenever \(a,b\in \Z\) such that \(p\mid ab\text{,}\) either \(p\mid a\) or \(p\mid b\text{.}\)

A natural number \(m > 1\) is said to be composite if it is not prime.

This is almost certainly not the definition of prime that you are familiar with from your school days. However, it is often more useful than the usual definition. And, as Theorem 1.3.2 demonstrates, the two notions are equivalent.

Theorem 1.3.2.

Given any \(p\in \N\text{,}\) \(p > 1\text{,}\) \(p\) is prime if and only if whenever \(m\in \N\) divides \(p\text{,}\) either \(m = p\) or \(m = 1\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(p\) be prime and suppose \(m\in \N\) divides \(p\text{,}\) so \(p = mk\) for some \(k\in \N\text{.}\) By definition, \(p|m\) or \(p|k\text{.}\) If \(p|m\text{,}\) then \(pj = m\text{,}\) so \(p = pjk\text{,}\) and \(jk=1\text{,}\) which means \(j=k=1\) and thus \(m=p\text{.}\) If \(p|k\text{,}\) then \(pj=k\text{,}\) so \(p = mpj\text{,}\) and \(mj=1\text{,}\) so \(m = j = 1\text{.}\)

Conversely, assume \(p\in \N\) has the property that whenever an integer \(m\) exists with \(m|p\text{,}\) then \(m= p\) or \(m =1\text{.}\) Suppose \(p|ab\text{,}\) so there exists a \(k\in\N\) such that \(pk = ab\text{.}\) Note that if \(\gcd(a,p) = d > 1\text{,}\) then \(d|p\text{,}\) and so \(d = p\text{,}\) and then \(p|a\text{.}\) If \(\gcd(a,p) = 1\text{,}\) then by Bézout's Identity there are integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(ax + py = 1\text{,}\) so we may multiply by \(b\) to obtain \(abx + pby = b\text{,}\) whence \(p|b\text{.}\)

Exploration 1.3.1.

Using Theorem 1.3.2 as a guide, give a biconditional characterization for composite numbers. That is, finish the sentence: “A number \(m\in\N\) is composite if and only if ....”

Answer

“A number \(m\in\N\) is composite if and only if there exist natural numbers \(a,b\ne 1\) such that \(m = ab\text{.}\)”

Theorem 1.3.3.

Let \(a\in \N\) such that \(a > 1\text{.}\) Then there is a prime \(p\) such that \(p\mid a\text{.}\)

Proof.

We proceed by mathematical induction. Note that when \(a = 2\text{,}\) the statement holds.

Assume that there is a \(k\in\N\) such that for all \(a\le k\text{,}\) the statement holds. Consider \(k+1\text{.}\) If \(k+1\) is prime, we are done. If not, then \(k+1\) is composite, and by the answer to Question 1.3.1, there are integers \(a,b\ne 1\) such that \(k+1 = ab\text{.}\) By induction, there is a prime \(p\) such that \(p|a\text{,}\) so \(p|k+1\text{.}\)

Our first major theorem makes two claims: that positive integers greater than 1 can be factored into products of primes, and that this factorization can happen in only one way. As the semester progresses, we will see other theorems like this one, and catch glimpses of other ways to think about the unique factorization property.

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

Every natural number greater than 1 is either a prime number or it can be expressed as a finite product of prime numbers where the expression is unique up to the order of the factors.

The proof is broken into two parts: existence (Theorem 1.3.4) and uniqueness (Theorem 1.3.6).
Theorem 1.3.4. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic–Existence Part 1 .

Every natural number \(n > 1\) is either a prime number or it can be expressed as a finite product of prime numbers. That is, for every natural number \(n > 1\text{,}\) there exist primes \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_m\) and natural numbers \(r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_m\) such that

\begin{equation*} n = p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots p_m^{r_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Hint

Use the Well-Ordering Principle (or something equivalent to it.)

Proof.

It is enough to show that, if \(n > 1\text{,}\) we may write \(n = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k\text{,}\) where the \(p_i\)'s are not necessarily distinct primes. Then we can collect the common primes and write them with exponent notation.

Note that when \(n = 2\text{,}\) there is such an expression, so let \(k > 2\) be the least positive integer that fails to be expressed as above. We note that \(k\) cannot be prime, or it could be expressed as above. Thus, by Theorem 2.1, there is a prime \(p\) such that \(k = p b\text{,}\) where \(1 \lt p, b \lt k\text{.}\) Since \(k\) was the least positive integer that cannot be factored as a product of primes, \(b\) has a prime factorization. But then so does \(k\text{,}\) a contradiction.

Lemma 1.3.5.

Let \(p\) and \(q_1, q_2,\ldots, q_n\) all be primes and let \(k\) be a natural number such that \(pk = q_1 q_2 \ldots q_n\text{.}\) Then \(p = q_i\) for some \(i\text{.}\)

Proof.

We first state a claim that will be useful.

Claim 1: If \(p,q\) are primes such that \(p|q\text{,}\) then \(p = q\text{.}\)

Proof of Claim 1: Since \(p, q > 1\) and both \(p\) and \(q\) are prime, \(p = q\) by the definition of primality. \(\checkmark\)

Now assume that \(pk = q_1 q_2 \cdots q_n = (q_1 q_2 \cdots q_{n-1}) q_n\text{.}\) If \(p|q_n\text{,}\) we're done by Claim 1. If not, the definition of a prime guarantees that \(p| q_1 q_2 \cdots q_{n-1}\text{.}\) If \(p|q_{n-1}\text{,}\) we're done by Claim 1. Otherwise, \(p|q_1 q_2 \cdots q_{n-2}\text{.}\)

Repeating this process, we see that either \(p|q_i\) for some \(i > 2\text{,}\) or \(p|q_1 q_2\text{.}\) Now the definition of a prime guarantees that \(p|q_1\) or \(p|q_2\text{,}\) in which case Claim 1 requires that \(p = q_1\) or \(p = q_2\text{.}\)

Theorem 1.3.6. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic–Uniqueness Part.

Let \(n\) be a natural number. Let \(\set{p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_m}\) and \(\set{q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_s}\) be sets of primes with \(p_i\ne p_j\) if \(i\ne j\) and \(q_i\ne q_j\) if \(i\ne j\text{.}\) Let \(\set{r_1,r_2,\ldots,r_m}\) and \(\set{t_1,t_2,\ldots,t_s}\) be sets of natural numbers such that

\begin{align*} n \amp = p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots p_m^{r_m}\\ \amp = q_1^{t_1} q_2^{t_2} \cdots q_s^{t_s}\text{.} \end{align*}

Then \(m = s\) and \(\set{p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_m} = \set{q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_s}\text{.}\) That is, the sets of primes are equal but their elements are not necessarily listed in the same order (i.e., \(p_i\) may or may not equal \(q_i\)). Moreover, if \(p_i = q_j\text{,}\) then \(r_i = t_j\text{.}\) In other words, if we express the same natural number as a product of distinct primes, then the expressions are identical except for the ordering of the factors.

Hint

Argue that the two sets are equal. Then argue that the exponents must also be equal.

Proof.

Without loss of generality, assume \(p_1 \lt p_2 \lt \cdots \lt p_m\) and \(q_1 \lt q_2 \lt \cdots \lt q_s\text{.}\) Given a \(p_i\text{,}\) we know that \(p_i | q_1^{t_1} q_2^{t_2} \cdots q_s^{t_s}\text{,}\) which implies that \(p_i | q_j\) for some \(j\) by Claim 2 in the proof of Lemma 2.8. Further, Lemma 2.8 implies that \(p_i = q_j\text{,}\) and similarly, given a \(q_j\text{,}\) \(q_j = p_i\) for some \(i\text{.}\) Thus, \(m =s\text{,}\) and by the ordering of the \(p_i\)'s and \(q_j\)'s, we have \(p_i = q_j\text{,}\) \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, m\text{.}\) Therefore,

\begin{equation*} n = p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots p_m^{r_m} = p_1^{t_1} p_2^{t_2} \cdots p_m^{t_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now, assume by way of contradiction, that \(r_i \ne t_i\) for some \(i\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, we may assume \(r_i \lt t_i\text{.}\) Then \(p_i^{t_i} | p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots p_m^{r_m}\text{,}\) which implies that

\begin{equation*} p_i^{t_i - r_i} | p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots \hat{p}_i^{r_i} \cdots p_m^{r_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(t_i - r_i > 0\text{,}\) we have that

\begin{equation*} p_i | p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots \hat{p}_i^{r_i} \cdots p_m^{r_m}\text{,} \end{equation*}

from which \(p_i | p_j\) for some \(j\ne i\) by Lemma 2.8. This is a contradiction, so \(r_i = t_i\) for \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, m\text{.}\)
